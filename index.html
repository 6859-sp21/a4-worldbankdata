India's growth story since 1960

I downloaded world bank datasets to broadly understand how India has performed over the years in certain parameters related to economics, education, and science and technology. These datasets are titled: “economy-and-growth”, “education” and “science-and-technology”. I do a comparitive assessment of India with its neighbour China, which was in a similar situation in the 60s.



Note: Please try to zoom in/out to adjust the visualizations to your screen

In early 1990s, both India and China underwent many economic reforms and both have made a huge progress since. However, the growth in China’s Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is much larger than India’s and that naturally begs the question: HOW?




The negative correlation does not imply that India’s GDP grows when its net trade of goods is negative. It possibly says that India’s GDP would’ve been higher had this value been positive. GDP is a complex combination of many factors, some of which have been shown in the previous plots. However, exported goods did indeed play a big role in China’s massive GDP growth and India, clearly, is a net importer of goods.






Another key factor that was observed was the heavy investment in science and technology. China has seen an unprecedented rise in the number of patents filed since the early 90s. In fact, a positive correlation can be seen between the number of patents filed and the GDP growth over years for some other nations as well.

I next plotted a sum of total GDP between 2016 and 2017 and a sum of total patents filed between the same period. A positive correlation can again be seen. Some that stands out is how China has overtaken everyone, highly likely due to its investments in science and technology.



Isn't India a better Service economy than China?

These plots show that India is better than China in terms of export of ICT services and not services in general. The image about India being better than China is thus built largely upon the ICT sector. However, even in that both India and China are quite closely placed as can be seen.

Finally, I compared similar trends across some other nations to ensure that China and India are not behaving anomalously and similar behavior can be seen in general. This was essential to make logical conclusions from the exploratory analysis. For instance, we can see that the number of patents filed by japan and its GDP curve almost follow the same slope. We also see that as Japan’s net trade in goods dipped a little below zero around 2013-2014, its GDP also dipped. We see a downward trend in Japan’s number of patents filed starting 2005 and we also see a downward trend in the net import of goods by Japan starting 2005. The effects on the economy at large, i.e., GDP are expected to lag this effect as can be somewhat seen in the small but noticeable flattening of slope of Japan’s GDP around 2010. Similar correlations can be seen for the other countries shown in the plot as well.
